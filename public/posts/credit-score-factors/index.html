What Actually Affects Your Credit Score (And What Doesn't)

The 5 credit score factors with percentages explained. Common myths debunked. Quick wins to boost your score fast. Real numbers from someone who improved from 580 to 750.

My credit score dropped 47 points because I opened a new credit card. I remember the panic of seeing your score plummet over something that was supposed to help. But here’s what I learned: most people don’t understand how credit scores actually work. We worry about the wrong things and ignore the things that actually matter.

For years, I thought my credit score was this mysterious number controlled by forces beyond my understanding. I’d heard conflicting advice: closing credit cards hurts your score, checking your own credit report damages it, paying off debt quickly kills it. It was all so confusing.

Then I actually learned how credit scores work—the real mechanics, not the myths. And everything changed. Suddenly I could see exactly what to focus on and what to ignore. I could make deliberate changes and watch my score improve predictably.

If you’re working on paying off debt, your credit score matters. Not because you need a high score to be a good person, but because a better credit score means better interest rates, which means you pay less money on that debt. A higher score today can save you thousands of dollars tomorrow.

Here’s what actually moves your credit score and what doesn’t.

The 5 Factors That Actually Matter#

Your credit score is calculated from exactly five factors. Everything else you hear about is either a myth or a minor effect. Here are the five, in order of importance:

1. Payment History (35% of your score)#

This is by far the most important factor. Did you pay your bills on time? If yes, good. If no, very bad.

Here’s what counts:

  • On-time payments: Boost your score. Every single on-time payment shows you’re reliable.
  • Late payments: Drop your score. A payment 30+ days late counts. The later you are, the worse it gets. 60 days late is worse than 30 days late. 90 days late is serious.
  • Collections: Destroy your score. If an account goes to a collections agency, your score drops 50-100+ points.
  • Bankruptcy: The nuclear option. It damages your score for 7-10 years, but the damage decreases over time.

The math: If you have 20 payments on your credit report and you’ve been 30 days late once, that’s one late payment out of 20. That’s 5% late. This stays on your report for 7 years, but its impact decreases year by year.

Action: Make all payments on time. If you’re behind, get current immediately. A single on-time month won’t erase history, but it stops the bleeding and starts a new pattern.

2. Credit Utilization (30% of your score)#

This is the second most important factor: what percentage of your available credit are you using?

Here’s the principle: if you have a $5,000 credit limit and you carry a $4,000 balance, your utilization is 80%. That’s bad. If you carry a $1,000 balance, your utilization is 20%. That’s good.

The algorithm looks at your utilization per card AND your total utilization across all cards.

Example:

  • Credit Card 1: $2,000 limit, $1,500 balance = 75% utilization
  • Credit Card 2: $3,000 limit, $300 balance = 10% utilization
  • Total: $5,000 limit, $1,800 balance = 36% utilization

Your overall utilization is 36%, which is in the healthy range.

The goal: Keep your utilization below 30%. Ideally below 10%. This shows lenders you’re not desperate for credit.

Action: Pay down credit card balances. You don’t need to pay off the entire balance monthly (though it’s great if you can), but lower the reported balance. This is why paying off debt is such a powerful credit score move—it directly lowers utilization.

3. Length of Credit History (15% of your score)#

This is how long you’ve had credit accounts. The longer, the better. You’re proving you can manage credit over time.

What counts:

  • Age of oldest account: The longer, the better.
  • Age of newest account: New accounts hurt slightly because they lower your average age.
  • Average age: This is what’s calculated.

Example:

  • Credit card opened 2010 (16 years old)
  • Credit card opened 2015 (11 years old)
  • Credit card opened 2023 (1 year old)
  • Average age: (16 + 11 + 1) ÷ 3 = 9.3 years

That’s healthy. The 2023 card lowered the average, but the old accounts more than compensate.

Action: Keep old accounts open. Don’t close your oldest credit card. Don’t close accounts just because you paid off the balance. Let them age. Don’t open new accounts unless necessary.

4. Credit Mix (10% of your score)#

This is variety—do you handle different types of credit responsibly?

Types of credit:

  • Revolving: Credit cards, lines of credit. You borrow, you repay, you can borrow again.
  • Installment: Auto loans, personal loans, mortgages. You borrow a set amount and repay in fixed payments.

Having both types shows you can manage different credit scenarios.

Example:

  • Credit card (revolving)
  • Auto loan (installment)
  • Credit card (revolving)
  • Student loans (installment)

This mix is healthy. It shows you’re not just relying on one type of credit.

Action: Having multiple types of credit is good, but don’t open new accounts just for the sake of credit mix. That’s what you do once you’ve paid off debt and want to optimize your score. While paying off debt, ignore this factor.

5. New Credit (10% of your score)#

This is inquiries and new accounts. Hard inquiries (when a lender checks your credit) and new accounts both count.

Here’s what matters:

  • Hard inquiries: When you apply for credit, a hard inquiry appears. Multiple inquiries in a short period look like desperation to lenders. One inquiry drops your score about 5-10 points, but the effect fades over 6-12 months.
  • New accounts: Opening a new account drops your score slightly because it lowers your average age of accounts and creates an inquiry. But the negative effect is temporary.

Example:

You apply for 5 credit cards in one month. That’s 5 hard inquiries. Your score drops 25-50 points temporarily. But rate shopping for auto loans or mortgages within 14 days counts as one inquiry (lenders know you’re comparing rates, not applying desperately).

Action: Only apply for credit when necessary. Don’t open cards just to chase rewards or a signup bonus if you don’t need credit. Each application is a temporary hit, even if it’s minor.

Common Myths Debunked#

Myth 1: Checking your own credit report damages your score.

False. Checking your own credit is a “soft inquiry” and doesn’t affect your score. Check it as often as you want at annualcreditreport.com. This is the official free service that lets you pull your report once per year from all three bureaus.

Myth 2: Closing a credit card improves your score.

False. Closing a card usually hurts your score because it lowers your available credit and increases your utilization percentage. Keep old cards open (but unused if they have annual fees).

Myth 3: Paying off all debt immediately will boost your score.

Partially false. Paying off debt lowers your utilization, which helps. But the bureaus like to see some balance reported. If you have a credit card with a $5,000 limit, having a small balance ($50-100) reported and then paying it off monthly is better than a $0 balance reported. However, if you’re carrying high balances across multiple cards, paying that down is absolutely the right move.

Myth 4: Paying off collections will remove them from your report.

False. Paying a collection account stops the bleeding (no more damage), but it stays on your report for 7 years. However, newer scoring models treat paid collections much better than unpaid ones. So paying is still worth doing.

Myth 5: Your income affects your credit score.

False. Credit bureaus don’t have your income data. They don’t care if you make $30,000 or $300,000. Credit is about payment history and debt management, not income.

Myth 6: You need to carry a balance to build credit.

False. You don’t need to pay interest to build credit. Pay your balance in full, on time, every month. The card still reports to bureaus and builds your credit. You’re just not throwing money away on interest.

Quick Wins to Boost Your Score Fast#

If you want to improve your score fast, here’s what actually works:

Quick Win 1: Get current on all late payments (Week 1)

If you have any late payments, getting current is priority #1. One on-time month won’t erase history, but it stops the bleeding and starts a positive trend. Your score might jump 20-50 points just from getting current.

Quick Win 2: Pay down credit cards to under 30% utilization (Week 2-4)

If you have $15,000 in credit limits and $12,000 in balances, pay enough to get to under $4,500. This could jump your score 50-150 points depending on how much you lower utilization.

Quick Win 3: Dispute errors on your credit report (Week 3)

About 20% of credit reports have errors. Check your report at annualcreditreport.com. See something wrong? Dispute it. Removing a hard inquiry or a reporting error can boost your score 20-50 points.

Quick Win 4: Request that recent hard inquiries be removed (Week 4)

Call the creditor who pulled your report. Politely ask them to remove the inquiry since you didn’t end up applying or were just checking your rate. Some will do it. Removing one inquiry can boost your score 5-10 points.

Quick Win 5: Ask a creditor to report a lower balance (Week 4)

Some creditors report balances as of specific dates. If you paid down a card mid-month and they report the balance on the 5th, call and ask them to update the reported balance. This can immediately lower your utilization.

Timeline for Score Improvement (Real Expectations)#

Here’s what realistic improvement looks like:

Week 1-2: Getting current on late payments: +20-50 points Month 1-2: Paying down credit cards: +50-150 points Month 2-3: Fixing credit report errors: +20-50 points Month 3-6: Consistent on-time payments: +50-100 points Month 6-12: Continued low utilization and on-time payments: +50-100 points

Total potential improvement in 12 months: 190-450 points

The exact improvement depends on your starting point. Someone improving from 550 to 650 might see different percentage jumps than someone improving from 680 to 750, but the process is the same.

Why This Matters for Debt Elimination#

Here’s the practical connection: when you pay off debt, your credit score improves. When your credit score improves, you qualify for better interest rates. Better interest rates mean you pay less money on future debt—whether that’s credit cards or loans.

If you’re carrying credit card debt at 18% APR and you pay it off, improving your credit score, you might refinance with a 12% APR loan. You’re paying 6% less interest. On $10,000, that’s $600/year saved.

That money could pay off debt faster. That faster payoff lowers interest even more. It’s a virtuous cycle.
