YNAB (You Need A Budget) – The Cult Classic#

What It Actually Does#

YNAB is built on four rules that force you to give every dollar a job before you spend it. In 2026, version 7.6 added native multi-currency support and a long-awaited joint-account “Household” tier that finally lets spouses share categories without merging logins.

Pricing in 2026#

  • Monthly: $14.99
  • Annual: $109 (effectively 3 months free)
  • Student: Still free for 12 months with .edu email

Real Pros#

  • Zero-based budgeting on autopilot: Once you trust the method, overspending is almost impossible.
  • Age of Money > 30 days: Gamified metric that keeps you one month ahead—psychologically huge.
  • Direct import + manual fallback: If Plaid hiccups (and it still does), you can drag-and-drop CSVs without breaking the budget.
  • Shared Household tier: My wife and I each have our “fun money” categories that the other can’t mess with.

Honest Cons#

  • Learning cliff: The first 30 days feel like learning a new language. Expect YouTube rabbit holes.
  • Price creep: $109/year isn’t Netflix money, but it is $1,308 over a decade.
  • No bill pay: You still need your bank’s app to push payments.

Who YNAB Is Actually Best For#

  • Couples who argue about money and need guardrails.
  • People with variable income (freelancers, realtors, creators).
  • Anyone who wants behavior change, not just tracking.

My Take#

YNAB is the Peloton of budgeting apps—expensive, evangelical, but it works if you commit. I used it for 6 straight years and still recommend it to friends who hate spreadsheets.

Mint – The Free Giant (RIP, But Still Kicking)#

What It Actually Does#

Mint is the OG aggregator. You link accounts, it auto-categorizes, and spits out pretty pie charts. In 2026, Intuit finally killed the classic Mint app and migrated users to “Credit Karma Money” (yes, the name is confusing). The core Mint experience now lives inside Credit Karma with ads and credit-score upsells.

Pricing#

  • Free, but you’re the product. Expect credit-card and loan offers peppered throughout the feed.

Real Pros#

  • Zero dollars: Hard to beat free.
  • Automatic everything: Link accounts once, and you’re done.
  • Credit-score monitoring: Weekly TransUnion updates baked in.

Honest Cons#

  • Ad fatigue: Every third screen is a “pre-qualified” loan offer.
  • Category chaos: It still thinks Chick-fil-A is “Fast Food” and Target is “Shopping – General.” Fixing 60 transactions a month gets old.
  • No manual transactions: If your bank isn’t supported (lookin’ at you, local credit union), you’re out of luck.
  • Sunset anxiety: Intuit has already sunset once; they’ll do it again.

Who Mint Is Actually Best For#

  • People who want a one-time snapshot of net worth.
  • Lazy budgeters who refuse to pay for software.
  • Users who don’t mind ads and just need “good enough.”

My Take#

I keep Mint/Credit Karma around for the credit-score alerts and nothing else. If you need granular control, look elsewhere.

Tiller – The Spreadsheet Wizard#

What It Actually Does#

Tiller feeds daily bank transactions into Google Sheets or Excel and auto-categorizes with user-defined rules. In 2026, they dropped a killer add-on called “Tiller Intelligence” that builds pivot tables and charts with natural-language prompts (“Show me my grocery spend last 90 days vs 2025”).

Pricing#

  • Monthly: $9.92 (billed annually at $119)
  • 30-day free trial: No credit card required

Real Pros#

  • Full spreadsheet freedom: Want to mash up crypto prices and grocery spend? Easy.
  • Custom categories without limits: I have 73. YNAB caps at 200; Mint forces its own.
  • Community templates: The Tiller community shares everything from FIRE calculators to Christmas sinking-fund sheets.
  • No vendor lock-in: Cancel anytime; your data lives in your Google Drive.

Honest Cons#

  • Spreadsheet comfort required: If =SUMIFS scares you, Tiller will feel like homework.
  • No mobile native app: You’ll use Sheets on your phone, which is… fine.
  • Bank lag: Yodlee sometimes takes 8–12 hours longer than Plaid.

Who Tiller Is Actually Best For#

  • Spreadsheet nerds who want automation without losing control.
  • Side-hustlers tracking multiple income streams.
  • Anyone who wants to build a custom dashboard (I made one with real-time dividend yields).

My Take#

Tiller is my daily driver now. I pair it with my own Excel templates (see below) for clients who refuse subscriptions.

Affiliate link: Start Tiller free for 30 days.

EveryDollar – The Ramsey Way#

What It Actually Does#

Built by Ramsey Solutions, EveryDollar is zero-based budgeting stripped to the essentials. The free version is manual entry only; the paid tier adds bank sync and group coaching calls.

Pricing#

  • Free: Manual only
  • Ramsey+ (includes EveryDollar Premium): $17.99/month or $139.92/year

Real Pros#

  • Dead simple interface: Add income, drag sliders to categories, done.
  • Ramsey method baked in: Baby Steps tracker is front and center.
  • Premium coaching: Weekly livestreams with Ramsey coaches included.

Honest Cons#

  • Manual entry pain (free tier): I lasted 6 days before rage-quitting.
  • Pricey for what you get: $18/mo is more than YNAB and way more than Tiller.
  • Limited customization: Can’t split transactions across categories in free tier.

Who EveryDollar Is Actually Best For#

  • Dave Ramsey superfans who want official endorsement.
  • Church groups doing FPU classes together.
  • People who prefer phone coaching over forum help.

My Take#

EveryDollar is the iPhone of budgeting apps—pretty, locked down, and expensive. If you’re already drinking the Ramsey Kool-Aid, go for it. Otherwise, pass.

Head-to-Head Deep Dive#

Ease of Setup (1 = painful, 5 = brain-dead)#

  1. Mint: 5 – Link accounts, done.
  2. EveryDollar: 4 – Manual but intuitive.
  3. YNAB: 3 – Need to learn the method first.
  4. Tiller: 2 – Must authorize Google Sheets and tweak rules.

Bank Connectivity Reliability#

  • YNAB: Plaid + MX redundancy, 99% uptime in 2026.
  • Mint/Credit Karma: Plaid only, occasional 2FA loops.
  • Tiller: Yodlee, slower but stable.
  • EveryDollar: Manual free tier, MX in paid tier (solid).

Reporting Depth#

  • Tiller: Infinite (you code it).
  • YNAB: Built-in reports + Toolkit extension.
  • Mint: Basic pie charts, no custom date ranges.
  • EveryDollar: Baby Steps only.

Customer Support#

  • YNAB: 24/7 chat, killer YouTube library.
  • Tiller: Email, 1-day turnaround, private Facebook group.
  • EveryDollar: Phone + live chat (Ramsey+ only).
  • Mint: None (community forums only).

Real-Life Scenarios#

The Freelancer with Variable Income#

Use YNAB. Rule 4 (“Age your money”) smooths feast-or-famine cycles. I lived on last month’s invoices while building my agency.

The Couple Who Can’t Agree on “Fun Money”#

Use YNAB Household tier. Separate logins, shared categories, no more Venmo wars.

The Spreadsheet Addict#

Use Tiller + my Etsy templates. Build whatever your nerd heart desires.

The Dave Ramsey Church Group#

Use EveryDollar Premium. Built-in FPU integration keeps everyone on the same page.

FAQ – 2026 Edition#

Q: Is Mint dead? A: Sort of. The classic app is sunset; the experience lives inside Credit Karma. Same data, more ads.

Q: Can I run YNAB and Tiller together? A: Yes. I export YNAB CSVs into Tiller for custom charts. Overkill? Maybe. Satisfying? Absolutely.

Q: What about Monarch, Copilot, Rocket Money? A: Honorable mentions, but none cracked my top 4 for 2026. Monarch is $14.99 and still missing split transactions; Copilot is iOS-only; Rocket Money is 90% subscription-cancel service.

Bottom Line#

If you want behavior change and don’t mind paying, get YNAB.
If you want spreadsheet freedom, get Tiller.
If you want free and lazy, use Mint inside Credit Karma.
If you want Ramsey branding, get EveryDollar.

If you hate subscriptions, grab my Etsy Excel templates and call it a day.
