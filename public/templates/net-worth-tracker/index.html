Frequently Asked Questions#

Q: How often should I update my net worth? A: Monthly is ideal for most people. Update balances on the same day each month (like the 1st) for consistent tracking.

Q: What counts as an asset? A: Anything of value you own: bank accounts, investments, retirement accounts, home equity, vehicles, valuable personal property.

Q: Should I include my home’s value? A: Yes, but be conservative. Use recent comparable sales or a conservative estimate, not Zillow’s high estimate. Subtract your mortgage in liabilities.

Q: How do I track joint finances with a partner? A: Add all shared assets and debts to get household net worth. You can also maintain separate columns if you want to track individual contributions.

Q: Do I include retirement accounts like 401(k) and IRA? A: Yes. Include their current balances in assets so your net worth reflects long-term savings.

Q: How should I value vehicles? A: Use a conservative current market value and update it periodically as the vehicle depreciates.

Q: Can I track business assets and debts? A: Yes. Add a separate category for business assets and liabilities if you want a full picture of your total net worth.

How It Works#

  1. List all assets such as checking, savings, investments, retirement accounts, and property values.
  2. List all liabilities including mortgages, student loans, car loans, and credit cards.
  3. Update balances monthly to create a consistent snapshot for each month.
  4. Review the net worth chart to see trends and progress over time.
  5. Adjust categories as needed to reflect changes like new accounts or paid-off debts.

What Makes This Different#

Net worth apps often pull data from multiple institutions but hide the details behind a dashboard. A spreadsheet is transparent. You can see every balance, edit every category, and decide how conservative your valuations should be. That transparency matters when you want a realistic view, not a glossy one.

Apps also tend to focus on short-term net worth without giving you a long-term record you control. With a spreadsheet, your history stays intact even if you switch banks, change apps, or decide to stop paying for a subscription. You can compare this year to last year without losing access to older data.

Spreadsheets are also flexible. You can add business assets, track home equity separately, or keep individual and joint totals side by side. That kind of customization is hard to find in a fixed app layout.

Real-World Example#

Assume your household has $9,500 in checking and savings, $32,000 in a 401(k), $8,000 in a Roth IRA, and a home valued at $280,000 with a $225,000 mortgage. You also have a $12,500 car loan and $2,300 in credit card balances. Your total assets are $329,500 and liabilities are $239,800, for a net worth of $89,700. When you update monthly, you can see how contributions to retirement accounts and extra mortgage payments increase your net worth over time. The chart makes progress clear even when the month-to-month changes are small.
