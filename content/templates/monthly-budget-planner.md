## How It Works

1. **Download and open the file** in Excel or Google Sheets. The template includes clear tabs for income, expenses, and summary views.
2. **Enter your income lines** for the month. Add each paycheck, side income, or reimbursements as separate lines so totals stay accurate.
3. **Log expenses as they happen** by category. The sheet totals each category and updates the monthly balance automatically.
4. **Review the summary and charts** to see where your money went and how much is left to allocate.
5. **Make quick adjustments** by editing categories, moving planned expenses, or adding savings goals for the rest of the month.

## What Makes This Different

Subscription budgeting apps are built for recurring fees and standardized categories. That works until your real life gets messy. With a spreadsheet, you decide the structure and keep it aligned with how you actually spend. If your bills change, you change the spreadsheet. No waiting on app updates, no locked-down categories, and no forced data entry flows.

Apps also rely on syncs, bank connections, and paid upgrades to unlock features. A spreadsheet gives you the full toolset on day one. You can track cash, split expenses, and run your budget offline. There is no surprise price increase and no monthly fee attached to basic features.

Most importantly, spreadsheets give you full data ownership. Your history stays in one file that you control, not on a platform that may sunset features or limit exports. If you want to compare this month to last year, your data is already there and easy to analyze. You can add notes, color code items, or build your own report without asking permission.

## Real-World Example

Let’s say your take-home income in April is $4,200 from two paychecks of $2,100 each. You plan fixed expenses of $1,450 for rent, $160 for utilities, $320 for car payment and insurance, and $260 for internet and phone. That leaves $2,010. You budget $600 for groceries and dining, $250 for gas and transit, $180 for health and prescriptions, $220 for household items, and $400 to savings. After logging those, the sheet shows $360 remaining, which you decide to split between a sinking fund for a future trip and a buffer for unexpected costs. The chart instantly shows that housing is 38 percent of your month, and you can see at a glance that groceries and dining are your biggest flex category.
