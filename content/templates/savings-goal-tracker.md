## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How many goals can I track?**
A: The template supports up to 10 simultaneous savings goals. Each goal has its own progress tracker and contribution log.

**Q: Can I track goals without deadlines?**
A: Yes. Target dates are optional. You can track open-ended savings goals without a specific deadline.

**Q: Does it calculate how much I need to save monthly?**
A: Yes. Enter your goal amount and target date, and the template calculates your required monthly contribution automatically.

**Q: Can I use this for a sinking fund?**
A: Absolutely. Many customers use this template to manage sinking funds for annual expenses, holidays, and irregular bills.

**Q: Can I track interest earned on savings?**
A: Yes. Add interest as a contribution entry so the progress bars stay accurate.

**Q: Can I split one goal across multiple accounts?**
A: Yes. Record deposits from different accounts in the contribution log and the total goal progress will update.

**Q: What if I pause contributions for a month?**
A: No problem. Leave that month blank and the tracker will still reflect the correct progress and timeline.

## How It Works

1. **Create each savings goal** with a name, target amount, and optional target date.
2. **Log every contribution** with date and amount so your progress stays accurate.
3. **Review monthly contribution guidance** if you want a consistent savings plan.
4. **Use the dashboard view** to see progress bars and totals for all goals in one place.
5. **Update goals as life changes** by adjusting dates or amounts and letting the math recalculate.

## What Makes This Different

Savings apps often require linking accounts and then pushing you into generic buckets. A spreadsheet lets you define goals in the language you actually use and track them without handing your data to another platform. You decide what counts as a goal and how progress should be measured.

Apps also tend to hide the underlying numbers behind progress widgets. With a spreadsheet you can see every contribution, edit it, and understand exactly how the totals are calculated. That clarity makes it easier to stay consistent and to explain goals to a partner.

Because spreadsheets are a one-time purchase, you avoid subscription costs that eat into your savings. And if you ever want to change banks or move money between accounts, your tracking system stays the same.

## Real-World Example

Suppose you set three goals: $6,000 for an emergency fund by December, $1,500 for holiday spending by November, and $2,400 for a summer trip by July. In January you deposit $300 to the emergency fund, $100 to holidays, and $200 to the trip. The tracker shows progress of 5%, 7%, and 8% respectively and suggests monthly contributions of $500, $150, and $400 to hit each target. In March you add a $50 interest credit to the emergency fund and the progress bar updates automatically. By seeing all three goals in one place, you can balance them without losing momentum.
