## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Does it update stock prices automatically?**
A: In Google Sheets, you can use the GOOGLEFINANCE function for automatic price updates. In Excel, you can manually update prices or use data connections.

**Q: Can I track crypto investments?**
A: Yes. Add any asset type including cryptocurrencies. You'll need to manually update crypto prices.

**Q: Does it calculate capital gains for taxes?**
A: The template tracks cost basis and current value, showing unrealized gains. For tax lot tracking and specific identification, you may need your brokerage's records.

**Q: Can I track multiple accounts?**
A: Yes. Tag each holding by account (401k, IRA, brokerage) and see allocation across all accounts combined.

**Q: Can I record regular contributions or deposits?**
A: Yes. Add contribution entries and update share counts so the performance view stays accurate.

**Q: Does it work for mutual funds with daily NAV pricing?**
A: Yes. You can update the current price manually or link a data source and the values will recalculate.

**Q: Can I set target allocation percentages?**
A: Yes. Add target percentages and compare them to current allocation to guide rebalancing.

## How It Works

1. **Enter each holding** with ticker, shares, and purchase price so the cost basis is correct.
2. **Update current prices** manually or with a data function in Google Sheets to refresh values.
3. **Tag each holding by account and asset class** to see allocation across all accounts.
4. **Log dividends and contributions** to track income and the impact of new deposits.
5. **Review the allocation and performance views** to spot rebalancing needs and monitor progress.

## What Makes This Different

Most investing apps focus on brokerage syncing and charts, but they rarely show the math. A spreadsheet gives you a clear view of cost basis, gains, and allocation in one place. You can adjust formulas, add custom asset classes, and see exactly how the numbers are calculated.

Apps also tend to be built around a single account. If you have a 401(k), IRA, and taxable brokerage, you often have to view them separately. In a spreadsheet, you combine everything and get a true household-level allocation without paying for a premium dashboard.

Finally, spreadsheets are durable. You can keep a long-term history of contributions, dividends, and balances without worrying about access limits or subscription changes. That makes it easier to track your investing journey year over year.

## Real-World Example

Assume you hold $8,000 in an S&P 500 ETF, $5,500 in a total bond fund, $3,200 in a tech stock, and $1,300 in cash. Your cost basis is $15,200 and the current value is $18,000, so your unrealized gain is $2,800. The allocation view shows 64% stocks, 30% bonds, and 6% cash. You set a target of 70% stocks, 25% bonds, and 5% cash, so you plan to direct your next $1,000 contribution into the stock ETF and reduce cash. The spreadsheet updates the allocation and shows how that change moves you closer to your target without needing any additional tools.
