## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is the 50/30/20 rule?**
A: It's a simple budgeting framework: 50% of income goes to needs, 30% to wants, and 20% to savings or debt payoff. This template automates the math.

**Q: Can I adjust the percentages?**
A: Yes. While the template defaults to 50/30/20, you can customize the percentages to fit your situation.

**Q: Does this work with irregular income?**
A: Yes. Enter your actual income each month and the template recalculates your budget limits automatically.

**Q: What's included in the download?**
A: You get both an Excel (.xlsx) file and instructions for using it in Google Sheets.

**Q: How do I decide what counts as needs versus wants?**
A: Needs are essentials like housing, utilities, groceries, insurance, and minimum debt payments. Wants are optional spending like dining out, travel, and entertainment.

**Q: Can I focus the 20% on debt payoff instead of savings?**
A: Yes. Many people use the 20% bucket for debt payoff, then shift it to savings once debt is gone.

**Q: Can I combine multiple income sources?**
A: Yes. Add all income streams and the template will allocate the totals across the three buckets.

## How It Works

1. **Enter your total monthly income** from all sources so the template has the right baseline.
2. **Review the 50/30/20 allocation** to see target amounts for needs, wants, and savings or debt payoff.
3. **Log expenses by category** and watch each bucket update as you spend.
4. **Adjust percentages if needed** to match your current goals or cost of living.
5. **Use the progress view** to see which bucket needs attention before the month ends.

## What Makes This Different

Many budgeting apps depend on bank connections and automated category guesses. A spreadsheet makes you decide what is a need versus a want, which builds better awareness. You see the rule in action instead of having an app make assumptions for you.

Apps also tend to charge for basic features or limit customization. With this spreadsheet, the percentages are yours to edit. If 50/30/20 does not fit, change it to 60/20/20 or 40/30/30 and the math still works. That flexibility matters as your income changes.

Spreadsheets also give you long-term continuity. You can keep each month in a single file, compare trends, and revisit old budgets without losing access when a subscription ends.

## Real-World Example

Imagine your take-home income is $4,000 this month. The template allocates $2,000 to needs, $1,200 to wants, and $800 to savings or debt. You budget $1,350 for rent and utilities, $350 for groceries, and $200 for insurance, leaving $100 in the needs bucket for other essentials. For wants, you plan $350 for dining out, $120 for streaming and subscriptions, $300 for travel savings, and $200 for hobbies. You put the full $800 toward a credit card balance. As the month goes on, the sheet shows when you are close to hitting your wants limit so you can make small adjustments before you go over budget.
