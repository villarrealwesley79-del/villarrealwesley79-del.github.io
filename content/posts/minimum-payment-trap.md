## The Deceptive Math Behind Minimum Payments

Credit card companies don't want you to understand amortization. Let's expose the machinery:

### How Minimums Are Calculated

Typical formula: **1% of principal + monthly interest** (or a $25-35 floor). This means:

- At $3,000 balance (18% APR): $30 principal + $45 interest = **$75 payment**
- At $10,000 balance: $100 principal + $150 interest = **$250 payment**
- At $25,000 balance: $250 principal + $375 interest = **$625 payment**

You're mostly paying for yesterday's coffee with tomorrow's paycheck. The principal reduction? Barely makes a dent.

## The Three Balances That Break Budgets

### Case Study #1: The "Small" $3,000 Debt

My original mistake. Let's track with 2% minimum payments:

```text
Month | Balance | Payment | Interest | Principal | Remaining
1     | $3,000  | $60     | $45      | $15       | $2,985
12    | $2,850  | $57     | $42.75   | $14.25    | $2,836
120   | $1,800  | $36     | $27      | $9        | $1,791
```

After 10 years, you've paid **$5,400** (180% of original balance) with **$3,600** still owed. This isn't debt - it's renting your own money.

## The Amortization Truth They Hide

Let's dissect a $10,000 balance at 18% APR with $200 minimum payments:

| Month | Payment | Interest | Principal | % Going to Interest |
|-------|---------|----------|----------|----------------------|
1       | $200    | $150     | $50      | 75%                  |
12      | $190    | $142     | $48      | 75%                  |
60      | $140    | $105     | $35      | 75%                  |
120     | $100    | $75      | $25      | 75%                  |

Notice the pattern? **75% of every payment covers past spending**, not current progress. This creates the illusion of movement while you're stuck in quicksand.

## The Psychological Trap: Why Your Brain Falls For It

Minimum payments exploit two cognitive biases:

### 1. The Anchoring Effect

That $200 number becomes your mental ceiling. Studies show people pay 29% less than optimal when shown minimum amounts. It's like saying "this much is acceptable" to your brain.

### 2. The Illusion of Control

Paying on time makes you feel responsible while the debt snowball barely moves. You're getting participation trophies for financial self-harm.

### 3. Mental Accounting

"I paid $2,100 this year" feels productive until you realize **$1,850** went to interest accrued during payments. You're paying yesterday's debt with tomorrow's income.
