## Self-Employment Tax Mechanics: 15.3% Explained

As a self-employed individual, you're responsible for paying both the employee and employer portions of payroll taxes, which include Social Security and Medicare taxes. This is why your tax rate is significantly higher than that of employees who are subject to payroll withholding.

The mechanics of self-employment tax can be complex, but it's essential to understand how it works:

- **Social Security Tax:** 12.4% of your net earnings from self-employment (up to a certain wage limit)
- **Medicare Tax:** 2.9% of your net earnings from self-employment
- **Total Self-Employment Tax:** 15.3% of your net earnings from self-employment

This tax rate applies to both the employee and employer portions of payroll taxes, which is why you'll pay a total of 15.3% in self-employment tax.

## Quarterly Estimated Taxes: Timing is Everything

As a self-employed individual, you're responsible for making quarterly estimated tax payments to the IRS. This ensures that you have enough money set aside for federal income taxes and self-employment taxes throughout the year.

The due dates for quarterly estimated tax payments are:

- **April 15th** for January 1 – March 31
- **June 15th** for April 1 – May 31
- **September 15th** for June 1 – August 31
- **January 15th** (following year) for September 1 – December 31

Failure to make these payments can result in penalties and interest — so mark these dates now.

## Top 8 Freelancer Deductions: Save Big With These Expenses

As a freelancer, you're eligible for various deductions that can help reduce your taxable income. Here are the top 8 freelancer deductions:

1. **Home Office** — Cover the cost of a dedicated workspace, including rent or mortgage interest, utilities, and equipment.
2. **Vehicle** — Deduct the business-use percentage of your vehicle expenses: gas, maintenance, insurance, and depreciation.
3. **Software** — Cover the cost of software subscriptions and licenses used for business purposes.
4. **Meals** — Deduct meals related to business activities, such as client meetings or networking events.
5. **Equipment** — Include equipment purchases: tools, machinery, and inventory.
6. **Travel** — Deduct travel expenses related to business activities, including transportation, lodging, and food.
7. **Insurance** — Cover the cost of business insurance policies, including liability, property, and workers' compensation.
8. **Retirement Plan Contributions** — Contribute to a SEP-IRA or other retirement plans to reduce taxable income.

Using these deductions strategically can significantly reduce your April tax bill.

## Common Deduction Mistakes: Avoid These Pitfalls

Many freelancers leave money on the table — or worse, trigger audits — by making these common errors:

- **Not keeping accurate records:** Missing receipts and mileage logs = missed deductions and audit risk.
- **Overstating deductions:** Claiming excessive or personal expenses as business costs can result in penalties and interest.
- **Not tracking tax law changes:** Deduction rules shift year to year. Always verify against the IRS website or a tax professional.

The fix is simple: use a spreadsheet or template to track every business expense month by month. Don't wait until March to reconstruct a whole year.
